It’s sunday 14:42. The first sunny day in recent memory, enough motivation to get me out of my room to a nearby cafe with my laptop and to actually think about things. It’s only a two minute walk away already feels far better than lying on the floor at home. Tatami mats are lovely in theory, but when you’re scared to put furniture down because of dents, you end up spending far too much time on the floor. The café I’m in has an open-airy interior and windows that overlook a main road in Gifu. Along the roadside are warning signs that this area has an unusually high frequency of accidents.

What I came here to think about is the position I find myself in, a feeling I suspect a lot of people my age know well: *What the fuck do I do with my life?* Recently, I told my employer that I wouldn’t be recontracting with Gifu Prefecture for a second term as a CIR. Coworkers told me they were heartbroken. Two even sent long emails listing reasons I should reconsider and stay. And at a company *nomikai* (drinking party) one coworker actually did cry. The strength of their reactions caught me off guard to say the least.

(I don’t feel that I’m particularly exceptional at my job. Although I suspect that rather than being valued for outstanding skills, it’s really that in a field that requires spending a lot of time studying in a room alone, having some semblance of communication skills is exceedingly rare).

At first I was firm on my decision and didn’t even consider renewing. My day-to-day work isn’t thrilling, and that felt like reason enough. But as August approaches and the reality of having nothing planned looms closer, I can feel my heartrate rising and my shoulders becoming tighter day-by-day. Slowly, I started to reconsider. Maybe another year wouldn’t be so bad? There are parts of the job I enjoy, the rent is cheap, and it’s a decent chance to save money and most of all, I don’t have a clear plan for what to do if I leave. 

A childhood friend of mine, Iggy, and I often talk about a concept from the anime *Fullmetal Alchemist.* The idea is:

***Humankind cannot gain anything without first giving something in return; to obtain something, something of equal value must be lost*.** 

In the world of alchemy, this means you can’t transmute gold without a material of equal value. And in our world, it’s no different: a one-off payment for a meal is not only money being spent but hours hours of your life (or someone else’s) that you trade for it. The principle is (perhaps overly) simple, but when applied deliberately, it can help gain clarity on what it is that we stand to lose from a given decision.

  
Applied to my situation, the upsides of staying another year are obvious. The role is respected. I can save money. Improve my Japanese. Buy time to think about what’s next. And, maybe most tempting of all, have a confident answer when someone asks *“so, what do you do?”*. But what do I give up in exchange? A year of my time. A year of youth. A year when I could be experiencing something new instead of watching the clock until I can leave work, go to the gym, force myself through a workout, eat dinner, sleep, and repeat. Waiting for Friday just to spend the weekend lying on the floor of my room, mentally and physically rotting.

And I was going to consider renewing my contract because I was scared of admitting I don’t know what I’m doing? *FUCK* that. 

Everyone faces decisions where the upside of staying put must be measured against what we lose by not moving. A secure job, a predictable routine, or social approval might feel safe, but they cost time, energy, and exposure to new experiences that is (as is often stated) the only indispensible resource, the *one* resource that cannot be transmuted because there is almost nothing of equal value. 

Only 16% of the world’s population lives in a developed country. Of which roughly 50% attend higher education, of which only a tiny fraction go to top universities. Out of those people, how many can say they have a supportive family, a financial safety net, great health and strong friendships. In terms of probability, I’m *monstrously* lucky. What a waste of a blessed position it would be to spend time chasing status or money in a career I don’t enjoy instead of taking advantage of those opportunities to find a life where I can actually be happy.

The first exposure to a new experience or environment accounts for a disproportionate amount of the learning. That potential is what I lose by staying still. I came to the same conclusion two years ago, and it still holds: if your raft is stuck at sea, you can either sit around waiting for the wind to come and carry you somewhere, or you can grab the oar and start paddling in any direction. One of those options is far more likely to take you somewhere worth going. The question isn’t about whether you’re willing to take risks, it’s whether you’re aware of the cost you’re paying by staying still, the oar is always in your hands.
