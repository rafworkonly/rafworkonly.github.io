title: Playing an Assassin on Japanese TV

I’m waiting in the extras’ room somewhere deep inside the prefectural office complex in Gifu. Along the corridor are three rooms: one for the extras (just me), one for the cast (just one actress), and one for Hiroshi Abe, who seems to be sufficiently famous to warrant a private chamber. Since each of us has our own room, I think, I’m not so different from Hiroshi Abe, am I?

I’ve been here since two o’clock. I taught a Brazilian cooking class from eight this morning (separate work for the International Centre); and now it’s nearing four. I’ve already peeled so many layers of skin from my lips that they’ve started to sting. I can’t tell whether this is anxiety or not- is it pathological to worry about chapped lips when you’re about to appear on TV? Or is it really quite natural?

Every so often I stand and pace. The person who brought me here said, “Please wait a moment,” and hasn’t come back for nearly two hours. Eventually, a man enters accompanied by a few other staff members. Originally I assume he’s the director but maybe I’m wrong, he’s wearing an acrylic blue windbreaker and a beanie. He has the type of gangly frame and demeanor that makes him seem a bit like an untrustworthy spider. He doesn’t introduce himself, he just tells me to stand and looks my body up and down. He then asks my height, and tells me I’ll be playing a spy- “Think 007, but in China.” Then he leaves.

After another long period of waiting, someone comes to escort me to the wardrobe room next door. I’m told to undress, I hesitate for a moment, I’m in the middle of a room shared with the whole costume team and the actress from my scene. I think well, if this isn’t what they want me to do it’s on them. As I remove my shirt, one of the costume staff studies me for a moment and says, without inflection, “You’ve got an impressive body, don’t you.”

I try on five shirts that are all too tight, despite having sent my measurements in advance. A voice crackles over someone’s walkie-talkie: the director is calling for me. Suddenly everyone is moving. I was meant to have been here much earlier.

They slick my hair back with gel and hairspray until it feels frozen to my scalp, a brittle helmet that feels as though it could be lifted off whole.

A prop gun is strapped to my side and I’m forced into a tiny and very constrictive blazer. Then I’m hustled into an elevator.

Inside, perhaps ten people are apologising to some higher-up for forgetting me. The doors open onto the twentieth floor: a corridor wrapped around a central pillar and floor-to-ceiling glass revealing the night skyline of Gifu. There are no rival towers out here in the country; the building stands alone.

(The twentieth floor of the prefectural office.)
I’m rushed toward the set, my suit shoes clip-clopping. It’s a long wooden banquet table heavy with candelabras and platters of lobster and other luxuries sealed in cling film. The two lead actors are already seated and rehearsing. The director sits opposite them, broad and imposing especially for a Japanese man.

(I later learn he is the great-grandson of the man depicted on the ten-thousand-yen note and a retired rugby player.)

Behind him stand somewhere between fifty and a hundred staff members, holding the script in silence and completely intent on the scene. Three or four enormous cameras loom on cranes around the table. There is no explanation of what I’m meant to do. A phone is pressed into my hand. “Hai, dōzo.” A gesture: go ahead.

Everyone is completely serious. I want to announce that I’m not a professional actor, that I’m not even being paid, but I know it’s not appropriate. My legs tremble. I walk forward and hand the phone… to the wrong actor.

“No, no, NO! That’s all wrong.”

My memory of this point is actually not very clear, maybe from accumulated fatigue from working the eight hours before this, or from the stress of being in front of a television crew speaking a language I still struggle with. I know that soon after this we rehearse again but this time I have to walk from the end of a long corridor. I’m to wait around the corner until the actress shouts a line in Chinese instructing me to bring the phone. Then I turn the corner and walk twenty metres that feel like a runway, toward the cluster of cameras and staff.

We shoot the sequence again and again. One of the assistant directors, a cohort of young men orbiting the director, tells me I’m not merely a spy but an assassin. I should walk with the confidence of someone that could kill anyone in the room. I think to myself: I feel anything but confident.

All the assistants have a particular urgency about them, as though each is rehearsing not only the scene but his own imagined future as a director. I wonder: how many will get there?

(The fragment of the script I was sent)
Back in wardrobe, my gun is removed and my suit is changed.

Did it clash with my costume, or did the director decide that someone so visibly uncertain shouldn’t be armed on screen? I’m not sure. The actress, my superior in the fictional crime syndicate, sits while her makeup is retouched. I ask a crew member if he travels often for work. He lists countries from the past year: Mongolia, Korea, Taiwan, France, Finland, Iceland.

“Where had the best food?” I ask.

“Japan, of course,” he says, almost puzzled by the question.

After two months in Mongolia, he adds, he was tired of sheep stew. He asks about food in the UK, about fish and chips. I tell him I eat it only a few times a year. The actress emits a prolonged, incredulous “Eeeeh?” without turning her head as the makeup team continues working.

Dinner is announced. Chicken or white fish bento. I take the fish and enter the dining hall. None of the costume team are present, so I select a peripheral table, far enough from the centre to avoid accidentally displacing someone important. It’s difficult to tell how much the seating follows the logic of hierarchical placement, seat of honour furthest from the door, and how much it resembles a school cafeteria’s popularity rankings. Judging by the lack of conversation and the worn workwear at my table, it’s likely both.

After eating quickly, I retreat to my extras’ room. We return to filming, and I’m told to wait alone on a bench at the end of the corridor, opposite the window, between takes. The interior lights and the dark exterior turn the glass into a mirror. I sit leaning forward, elbows on knees, fingertips pressed together in a pyramid, examining the costume of my would-be villain. It feels like an appropriate pose.

Later, I’m permitted to wait in a back room while filming runs five hours over schedule. I ask the crew if it’s always like this. They tell me it’s always high stress. Members of the catering team are quietly eating cold prawns they pick out of a red sauce in zip-lock bags. Leftovers from the banquet display that I assume would otherwise be thrown away. When they notice me watching they grin and tell me not to tell anyone. The purplish-black shadows beneath their eyes are unmistakable.

As if I would.
