When I finished university, I was convinced that I must be immune from burnout. It's a concept that I had heard being discussed a lot during my studies, but at the time it felt quite abstract to me. I had done the all-nighters for exams, sat glued to my textbooks during movie nights, and studied every single day (apart from the 3 days per year that I'd allow myself to take off). Naively I had thought that if I was going to experience burnout then surely it would be during this period of my life. 

Despite this, I was quickly humbled once I started working full-time. Between the 2 hour round commute and adjusting to a new job, it wasn't long before my life felt consumed by my workplace. Maybe if the job itself was fulfilling and not terribly understaffed, it wouldn't have been so draining. As much as I adore my colleagues (and they've truly become some of my closest friends), they're no substitute for poor management and most days I would come from work and just spend the evening staring into space (or doomscrolling) as I try and decompress for the day.

The mental fatigue that I was experiencing began to encroach into my free time until I was too tired even make a simple decision about how to unwind. I started to feel hollowed out and disconnected from what made me a person outside of my 9-5. The things that I had once enjoyed or felt passionate about, I suddenly hadn't thought about all in weeks. 

At this point, I had to admit to myself that I was experiencing burnout. In fact, the whole experience made me realise that I had probably been suffering from burnout during university too, but it was too easy at the time to focus on the overwhelming workload rather than acknowledging this.

Coming to this realisation (which was hard for me to admit), I've been able to figure out a better work-life balance. I now actually utilise my time in the evenings and during the weekends to reconnect with the things that I care about. Even during the busy periods of my life, where I'd once be non-stop thinking about how desperately I want to rest, I'm able to be appreciative of the opportunities that I'm experiencing and the people I'm experiencing them with. 

What I've learnt, as simple as it is, is that it's genuinely important to make time for just yourself. It's a piece of advice that I've brushed off a hundred time, thinking that it didn't apply to me and that if I wasn't being productive then I was wasting time. Now I consider it lesson learnt. I know that this won't be the last time that I'm burnt out (not even close), but I hope that I never lose my sense of self again in the process.
