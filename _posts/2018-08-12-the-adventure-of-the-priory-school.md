title: The Weather Inside the Meeting Room
author: Raf

I scan the room for someone I know to sit beside, an inevitable private panic reserved for large gatherings. Looking for a familiar face whilst in the back of your mind a timer counts the seconds before you become that person, alone. Just as I feel the timer ticking down, I see the back of K’s frizzy afro. He’s taller than most of the four-hundred or so CIRs (Coordinators for International Relations) gathered here, and easy to find.

I make my way over and slip into the seat next to him whilst one of the event organizers reads out the items left behind from yesterday’s sessions: a pair of noise cancelling headphones, coloured contact lenses, a purple ballpoint pen. K and I exchange a few words about the previous day. He’s a south Londoner with the calm, mildly amused expression of someone who has learned to drift through meetings like passing clouds. Before becoming a CIR, he’d worked at Amazon, an experience that seems to have taught him that life is easier when you don’t stress over the rules. By day two he’d already swapped his suit shoes for Nike trainers, confident no word would make its way back to his contracting organisation.

The event we’re at is the mid-year training course for all of Japan’s CIRs, many of whom had to travel more than a day to get here as they are stationed in remote areas. It may well be the largest gathering of non-native Japanese speakers in the world, hundreds of us from dozens of countries, most of whom don’t speak English as a first language. This year’s training course is held in Ariake, Tokyo. An artificial island built atop ‘Tokyo Bay Landfill No. 10’, its urban planning is almost entirely dedicated to office space and business events of which one takes place almost everyday throughout the year. Dominating it all is ‘Tokyo Big Sight’, a steel-and-glass fortress of a convention centre. It looms over the surrounding parks below with a quiet menace, a monument to Japan’s relentless labour culture.

(On my way to the lecture, I picture a captured princess, or a giant spiked turtle, lurking somewhere in a top-floor meeting room.)

The day’s first lecture covers Japanese business etiquette. As is customary, the speaker begins with a self-introduction, emphasizing the word ‘Harvard’. But on the projector behind him, the program name shows clearly - a one year-long business course. He’s dressed in typical salaryman’s attire- a dark navy suit and a lack of patterns or texture that would make him stand out. In Japan there’s a saying ‘The nail that sticks up gets hammered down’. His hair has been brushed but perhaps in a rush and beneath his eyes are the dark circles the Japanese call ‘kuma’, (panda eyes).

‘Who thinks Japanese people are weird?’ he asks suddenly. ‘Raise your hand if you think they’re weird.’ As he talks, he intermittently forgets to hold the microphone close enough to his mouth and what should be an address to hundreds shrinks to a private conversation with the first few rows. The rest of us politely pretend to hear.

During the fifteen-minute break, I tell K that a lot of the CIRs I met that morning were grumbling about having to come all the way to Tokyo for the conference. I confess my surprise; I’ve been looking forward to this for weeks, an escape from the quiet of my desk. K agrees as we head out into the corridor.

He greets a sharply dressed Italian man with a single stud earring that introduces himself to me as Ricky (Riccardo maybe originally?). He met K yesterday in the smoking room, and the they announce they’re heading back in there now like it’s already been agreed that’s how they’ll use every break.

(Smoking rooms are common in Japanese offices and there are still many bars, cafes and hotels that permit smoking indoors although it’s becoming less common)

From the hallway the smoking room looks like it’s filled with fog. I hesitate before stepping inside, but I consider that turning around could seem like a commentary on the smokers themselves. The room is scattered with waist-high ashtrays around which CIRs and unrelated salarymen mingle in a soft grey. The smoking room is on the ninth floor and overlooks Tokyo Bay, but everyone seems too focused on the rest of their day inside the building to pay attention to the world outside.

Ricky launches into a story about being taken fishing on his first day at his placement, and how everyone was impressed with the size of the fish he caught. He gestures dramatically with his hands. I joke that fishing probably requires more skill from the fish than the fisherman, but he doesn’t seem to understand my tone and interprets it as downplaying his achievement.

Our next lecture is on copyright law. Apparently many CIRs have been using copyrighted material for their host organisation’s social media and getting scolded for it. I brace myself, hoping to stay awake.
