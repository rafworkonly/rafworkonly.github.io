At the start of August 2025, I began a new job as a ‘COORDINATOR of INTERNATIONAL RELATIONS’ (CIR) in Japan, a government position designed to introduce foreign cultures to local communities through cultural lectures, interpreting, translating, but seemingly most of all through their presence.

Japan has a long history of limited contact with the outside world. From the early seventeenth century for more than two hundred years, while the rest of the world grew steadily more connected, Japan carefully avoided foreign influence. Trade was restricted to a few ports, and ships were meticulously searched for foreign missionaries. Japanese citizens were forbidden from travelling abroad (even those who drifted overseas by mistake). Returnees were publicly decapitated, their headless bodies arranged on display on the hillsides and outskirts of towns, scarecrows to remind the people of Japan not to even consider engaging with the outside world.

Perhaps unsurprisingly, this historical emphasis on isolation left a lasting imprint: Japan prizes its traditions and sense of distinctness — sometimes so much that the outside world can feel vaguely unnecessary. Only 16.8% of Japanese people hold a passport, despite the country’s wealth and development. When Japanese people talk about travel, they usually mean domestic travel. In daily conversation, terms like 海外 kaigai (‘overseas’) or 外国人 gaikokujin (‘foreigner’) appear frequently, serving as indiscriminate labels for that which is not Japan. One small measure of that inward focus: Japan’s English ability ranks among the lowest in the developed world. In a recent study, Japan ranked 92nd out of 116 non-native English-speaking countries, significantly behind South Korea (49th), Vietnam (58th), Mongolia (76th), and China (82nd). (Of course, this data is somewhat biased since it relies on voluntary test participation, but it still gives a useful general picture.)

The longer I live here, the more I’m struck by how little curiosity there often is about the outside world. A Chinese colleague of mine, who speaks Japanese, recently went on a first date with a local firefighter. When the topic of calligraphy came up, he expressed genuine surprise that China also practiced it. My colleague politely explained that calligraphy had in fact originated in China, a detail that might not seem so obscure, given that Japan’s writing system itself is derived from Chinese characters. He was astonished. Later in the evening, when asked if he knew any Chinese words, he gleefully replied, and with commendably precise pronunciation, ‘annyeonghaseyo!’ (hello in Korean).

I tell this story because it explains why the government came up with a programme that pays someone like me, with relatively little professional skills, a salary (of around 4 million yen per year / £19,760) significantly higher than many long-serving and overtime-working local employees (which unsurprisingly seems to be a source of some resentment).

When I applied for this job almost a year ago, I had no say over where I would end up, placements were decided entirely by which cities happened to request a British CIR that year. A major city, or a remote island.

(One of my colleagues, an American bodybuilder, was placed on such an island and has become a local celebrity, partly for his impressive physique, partly for his surprisingly moving performances of traditional Japanese singing, of which he sells records. His profile picture on the work LINE groupchat is him shirtless draped in the American flag.)

My placement brought me to Gifu city, a modest regional capital in the Chūō region of central Japan. The city has a slowly declining population of roughly 300,000. My office, The Gifu International Centre, occupies the 2nd floor of a cube shaped building in an old shopping district called Yanagase (Literally ‘willow valley’). According to my older colleagues, only a few decades prior it was a lively shopping arcade, crowded with families browsing stalls filled with local crafts.

(Yanagase 1968)

These days it’s a different scene, on my walks to work, the streets feel almost post-apocalyptic, as most shops are closed or open only with staff that look bored at their lack of customers. Every evening, however, the former bustle of Yanagase is temporarily resurrected - not by families visiting the market, but by office workers drawn to the nightlife: Host clubs (where women pay men for attention), Hostess bars (where men pay women for attention), and sōpurando (ソープランド, soaplands) - brothels where clients are bathed before receiving sexual services.

(One of the entrances to Yanagase arcade)

(These billboards show the most popular hosts, ranked by how much money customers spend on them in alcohol each month.)

(The shops of Yanagase are often closed or open at very irregular times.)

By morning, that energy has disappeared once again, and a stillness seeps in through the air conditioning and into my office. The Gifu International Centre is tucked beneath the covered roof of the arcade, so the office gets very little natural light. Inside, it’s open-plan, with two islands of desks arranged so that the lowest-ranking employees sit nearest the door and the highest-ranking ones furthest away, at the head of the table.

(Not my office but very similar; picture this, but without the natural light.)

Throughout the day the office is almost silent. There’s no idle chatter; even whispers are measured, careful not to disturb the delicate atmosphere. When someone falls ill but doesn’t want to spend a precious paid holiday (there is no concept of sick leave) they arrive masked, sniffling or coughing quietly through the day, their sounds permeating the otherwise soundless room.

I arrive at least 15 minutes early everyday, (to arrive on time is seen as arriving too late) power on my laptop, open Outlook, and check whether I’ve been emailed any new tasks.

(My virtual work environment.)

Most mornings, there are one or two emails waiting: a translation request, an event to prepare for, or something I need to ‘confirm’. Office emails follow a particular rhythm. Every message begins with a greeting-「otsukaresama-desu 」(‘Thank you for your hard work’) and then the sender’s own name-「Takahashi-desu」 (‘This is Takahashi’)- even when it’s from someone sitting three desks away. Opening Outlook each morning has become something I find myself looking forward to, like checking an in-game mailbox in Stardew Valley. As if the system were gently reminding me who each character is, in case I’ve forgotten. Or perhaps it’s simply the novelty of reading such formal, strangely impersonal exchanges in a language that still feels half-coded to me.

(Stardew Valley’s mailbox. If the office had a similarly relaxing OST I might find work a lot more enjoyable.)

Some mornings, I wonder what this is really adding up to, especially knowing I’ll probably leave at the end of my one-year contract. But then again, maybe it’s noticing these small things that matters most.
