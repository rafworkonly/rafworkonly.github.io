I’m using a greasy cardboard bowl from tonight’s nabe to try to contain a particularly large cockroach clinging to the ceiling. I was warned to be mentally prepared for a lot of uninvited insects— my apartment is on the ground floor. Originally, when I pointed it out to my girlfriend, she was quick to give up hope of evicting this particular tenant. She glanced up, unimpressed.

“ii-n-janai?” -why not just leave it?

She grew up in Okinawa, Japan’s southernmost Island and I suppose must therefore be acquainted with tropical sized bugs, the kind I’ve only ever seen on Deadly 60.

I imagined going to sleep while it scuttled freely above me, or worse, across me; and promptly dismissed her suggestion.

I don’t need to stand on a chair to reach the ceiling; it’s not particularly high, though just tall enough that I have to fully extend my arm and stand directly beneath the disposable-lighter-sized creature. I can see its anatomy, its abdomen, or whatever the middle bit of an insect is called, and the faint tremor of its antennae sensing my movement, maybe even my fear?

Each time I edge the bowl closer, it darts sideways, evading me like the last piece of eggshell stuck in the white of a cracked egg. And now it’s directly above my futon. I take a breath and prepare to try again. I imagine the scenario in which it falls directly on my face. I’m not bad with bugs, but surely that’s a step too far for anyone.

This time, I manage to trap it, but the plaster above disintegrates under the cardboard, and clumps of grey-ceiling sift into my mouth and onto my pillow below. I really hope it’s not asbestos, and tactically decide not to research the matter, just in case it is. As I think this, I feel the weight of the roach rushing back and forth, all its legs tapping against the container in my hand. I carefully venture outside to set it free in the car park, hoping that cockroaches don’t have good navigation skills, and that it won’t find its way back inside.

This experience is not unique to me. My neighbours are all also prefectural government employees; all housed in the same fifty-year old building. Since its construction, safety regulations have changed so much that it probably couldn’t be built today. Luckily (or perhaps unluckily), Gifu has been spared major earthquakes, so the building remains standing.

The prefecture, I’m told, has no plans to rebuild the apartments. They’re waiting for nature to make the decision for them; an earthquake, a flood, some act of the universe that would settle the question of renovation (a gamble that might not last long anyway, given seismologists predicting a “mega-quake” within the next few years). Until then, it’s the tenants that maintain. We replace the tatami, we buy tape for the windows, air fresheners for the drains, and posters to cover the holes in the walls. It’s a modest, shared project: to live well inside something that has outlived its intended purpose.

At work, the logic isn’t so different. The centre’s website still bears the design of another decade; the same paper forms are printed, stamped, and passed from desk to desk, and a single typo means starting over. The intermittent clatter of the fax machine, a reminder of how little changes. All parts of a system waiting for a tremor strong enough to justify reform.

Recently, a colleague told me how long it had taken her to get permission to order event materials on Amazon instead of sourcing everything locally; even for an event about introducing foreign products. She told me to be grateful, and that things were much easier now than they used to be. I thought of all that time spent in meetings, arguing, risking her reputation with superiors, just to make the smallest improvement to a process that will remain inefficient by design. Maybe it’s best not to ask whether it’s worth trying to make a life comfortable within a system already tilting toward collapse, or whether comfort itself only delays the fall.

These signs are just more visible at home. The floor’s linoleum carries a rust-coloured stain in every panel, repeating like a low-resolution texture from an early horror game. The lightbulbs glow with a faintly blue even after they’re switched off; an eerie mystery, and surely a waste of energy.

I furnished the place from the recycling shop down the road; a dining table and chairs, a small washing machine, a mini fridge passed down from the local Japanese-Chinese friendship association. Over time, the space begins to feel lived-in, maybe even comfortable. I’ve covered the holes in the walls with posters and chosen a mud-coloured doormat that suits the room’s palette.

If I’m here for a year or two, I’m still just passing through. For many of my colleagues, that passing has already settled into living. I don’t have to make sense of it. They do. What does it matter, the choice of words you use to explain yourself to yourself? You might, to feel virtuous, say: I maintain. Or you might say: I am resigned. To maintain, to keep on making the effort, to push back against entropy. Or to be resigned, to know things are falling apart. But to go on anyway. Or something in between.

Maybe this is how outdated systems survive. Not because they are flexible and resilient, but because we are. The systems calcify and seem on the brink of crumbling, but through small compromises, quiet concessions, and the steady chipping of our ideals, we find ways to keep living inside them.
